How Much Protein Do You Actually Need Per Day?

PlateAI TeamJuly 10, 20266 min read

Protein is the macro everyone agrees matters and almost nobody hits consistently. Not because it's complicated — because the target most people are using is either outdated or was never meant for them in the first place.

The RDA is a floor, not a target

The commonly cited 0.8 g of protein per kilogram of bodyweight (about 0.36 g per pound) is the U.S. RDA — the minimum intake to prevent deficiency in a sedentary adult. It was never designed to optimize body composition, preserve muscle during weight loss, or support anyone who trains. Treating it as a daily goal is like using the minimum wage as a salary target.

For most active adults — and especially anyone in a calorie deficit — the more useful range sits considerably higher:

1.6–2.2 g of protein per kg of bodyweight (roughly 0.7–1.0 g per pound) covers nearly everyone from "generally active" to "training seriously," with higher end reserved for a calorie deficit.

Why protein needs go up when calories go down

This is the part most calorie-counting advice misses entirely. In a deficit, your body is looking for energy anywhere it can find it — and without enough protein coming in, some of that energy comes from muscle tissue, not just fat. Higher protein intake during a cut is one of the most consistent findings in body-composition research: it helps preserve lean mass while the scale drops, so more of the weight lost is actually fat.

Protein also carries the highest satiety value of the three macros — gram for gram, it keeps you fuller longer than carbs or fat, which matters enormously when you're eating in a deficit and hunger is working against you.

What that actually looks like on a plate

Numbers on a chart are easy to nod along to and hard to hit. Here's roughly what different amounts of common protein sources contain:

Chicken breast, 100g~31g protein
Greek yogurt, 200g~20g protein
Eggs, 2 large~12g protein
Salmon, 100g~25g protein
Cottage cheese, 200g~24g protein
Tofu, 150g~18g protein
Lentils, cooked 200g~18g protein
Whey scoop, 30g~24g protein

Hitting 130–160g a day usually means protein at every meal, not just one big serving at dinner — spreading intake across 3–4 meals also appears to support muscle protein synthesis better than front- or back-loading it into a single sitting.

Where people fall short without realizing it

  • Breakfast is usually the weakest meal — cereal, toast, and coffee can carry a whole day's worth of carbs and almost no protein.
  • "Healthy" doesn't mean high-protein. A salad with a light dressing can be under 10g of protein unless it's built around a real protein source.
  • Snacking skews toward carbs and fat by default — chips and crackers are effortless; a protein-forward snack usually takes planning.

None of this requires precision to the gram. Getting within 10–15g of your target most days will do more for how you look and feel than hitting an exact number occasionally. The goal is a number you can consistently land near — not a perfect one you abandon by Thursday.

This article is general educational information, not medical or dietary advice. Individual protein needs vary with age, kidney function, and training history — consult a qualified healthcare provider or dietitian for personalized guidance.
