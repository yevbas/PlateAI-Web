What the Health Score Actually Measures

PlateAI TeamJune 26, 20263 min read

Two meals can have identical calories and completely different health scores — which confuses people the first time they see it. That gap is the entire point. Calories tell you about energy; they don't tell you much about what a meal is actually made of.

It's not a calorie count wearing a different label

A 500-calorie plate of grilled salmon, greens, and quinoa is not nutritionally interchangeable with a 500-calorie serving of fries and a soda, even though your calorie total for the day would look identical either way. The health score exists to surface that difference — a single number that reflects the quality of a meal, not just its energy content.

Calories answer "how much energy." The health score answers "how well does this meal serve the rest of your body."

What actually goes into the number

The score blends several signals from the same analysis that generates your macros, weighted toward what the evidence on diet quality consistently points to:

Macros
Balance across protein, carbs, and fatA plate isn't scored on being "low-carb" or "low-fat" in isolation — it's scored on whether the macro mix is reasonably balanced for a single meal, rather than dominated by one macro at the expense of the others.
Nutrients
Density of fiber, vitamins, and mineralsTwo meals with the same calories can differ enormously in fiber and micronutrient content. Whole, minimally processed ingredients tend to score higher here than refined or heavily processed ones.
Portion
Signals from the photo itselfPlate coverage and apparent portion size feed into the estimate — a reasonable serving reads differently than one that visibly overshoots a typical meal.

Why it's a relative signal, not a grade

The most useful way to read the health score is comparatively — across your own meals, over time — rather than as a pass/fail grade on any single plate. A 6 today and an 8 tomorrow tells you something real about the shift in what you ate. A 6 in isolation doesn't mean you did something wrong; the occasional lower-scoring meal is normal and expected. It's the pattern across a week that's worth paying attention to, the same way the weight trend line matters more than any single day.

The score arrives after your macros, not instead of them

It's stage three of PlateAI's analysis — core macros land first, the health score follows once the fuller nutrient picture is in.
